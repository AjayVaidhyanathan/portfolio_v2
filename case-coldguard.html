The client

Priya Foods Distribution Pvt. Ltd. is a fictional regional distributor of meat, poultry, seafood, and frozen processed foods based in Madurai, Tamil Nadu. They supply 140+ hotels, supermarkets, and restaurants across southern Tamil Nadu from a 1,200 m² warehouse operating 24 hours a day.

At any given time, approximately ₹42 lakhs of perishable stock sits across 4 refrigerated rooms (2°C–8°C) and 2 deep freezers (−18°C to −22°C). Every degree of temperature exceedance is a direct financial risk. The margin between "safe" and "condemned" is measured in minutes, not hours.

The situation

Cold chain operations are unforgiving. A refrigeration failure at 2 AM that goes undetected until the morning inspection is not a minor inconvenience. It is stock destruction. Priya Foods had built their operations around three assumptions that were quietly failing them: that someone would notice a failure quickly, that two temperature logs a day was enough for compliance, and that staff awareness alone would keep doors properly sealed.

Power recovery time 8–15 min

From grid failure to generator running: someone had to notice, walk to the generator room, and start it manually. Deep freezers begin losing product integrity after 20 minutes.*

Compliance log frequency 2×/day

Temperature recorded manually at 6 AM and 6 PM. Everything that happened between those two readings was invisible to the operations team and to FSSAI on audit.

Confirmed major incidents 2 in 18 mo

One night-shift power failure undetected for 40 minutes. One silent compressor failure caught only at morning inspection. Combined loss: ₹8.9 lakhs in condemned stock.

Door-ajar events/month ~15

Estimated from post-incident analysis. Most caught within 30 minutes. Several ran 2–3 hours during night shift. A 2 cm gap on a refrigerated room door raises internal temperature ~1°C every 8–10 minutes.

"We knew something had gone wrong when the morning shift arrived. By then the damage was already done. There was no way to know sooner."

The real issue was that the operation had no continuous signal. Temperature data existed in a notebook twice a day. A power failure was only detectable if someone happened to notice the lights go out. The cold chain, the single most critical thing keeping the business running, was effectively unmonitored between manual checks.

* How we quantified the cost: a deep freezer at -20°C with the compressor off rises roughly 0.4°C per minute in a closed unit under Tamil Nadu ambient conditions (around 30°C outside). The safe threshold is -18°C. That's about a 5-minute window before breach, and product quality starts degrading at -12°C (minute 20). Modelling ₹6 lakhs of stock per unit against that degradation curve gives an estimated ₹10,000-₹12,000 loss per minute in a full failure. Total annual loss before ColdGuard was estimated at ₹21+ lakhs across power events, door incidents, compliance admin, and penalty risk.

The solution

ColdGuard is an IoT monitoring and alerting system built in partnership with NexSense IoT Solutions (fictional), a Chennai-based industrial IoT integrator. It monitors temperature, humidity, door seal status, and three-phase power on every unit, every 60 seconds, and gets an alert to the on-duty technician's phone within 90 seconds of a sustained breach.

The thinking behind it was simple: close every gap in the continuous signal. If the data is there, the problem gets caught before it becomes a loss event. The alert, the response, and the FSSAI log are all just downstream of having reliable data flowing without interruption.

01
Automatic Transfer Switch (ATS). The Socomec ATYS G replaces the manual transfer switch. On grid failure, it switches to generator power in under 200 milliseconds, before a compressor even has time to register a loss. The 8-15 minute manual recovery window is gone.
02
Continuous sensor monitoring. ESP32 microcontrollers paired with Sensirion SHT40 sensors (±0.2°C accuracy) and magnetic door contact sensors on every unit. One reading per minute, every minute, streamed via MQTT to the cloud. 1,440 data points per unit per day instead of 2.
03
10-minute rule alert engine. Alerts don't fire on a single out-of-range reading. That would just create noise during normal loading and unloading. The engine fires only when temperature stays above threshold continuously. The window is configurable per unit: 10 minutes for refrigerated rooms, 5 minutes for freezers, 3 minutes for the blast chiller.
04
FSSAI compliance, automated. The daily report that previously required a manual log, weekly data entry, and monthly email to the compliance officer now generates automatically at midnight and is delivered to the authority at 00:05 AM. The compliance officer's 4 hours per week on log management: zero.

A key resilience decision: the Raspberry Pi gateway runs the alert logic locally, not just in the cloud. If AWS connectivity drops, the gateway keeps logging to SQLite and fires local alerts over the on-premise network. The cold chain stays protected even when the internet doesn't. This came from a real concern the client raised early: they didn't want to swap a manual single point of failure for a cloud-dependent one.

Data flow

Understanding the data flow clarifies why the system can respond in under 90 seconds and why it can also produce a legally defensible compliance report covering every minute of every day.

Sense
ESP32 reads SHT40 sensor + door contact + power monitor every 60 seconds
Transmit
Publishes JSON payload to MQTT topic priyafoods/warehouse/unit_XX/sensor over Wi-Fi → Raspberry Pi gateway picks up and forwards to AWS IoT Core over TLS
Queue
AWS SQS buffers incoming messages, decoupling sensor volume spikes from the processing layer
Evaluate
Lambda function checks each reading against unit thresholds. Queries last N readings from TimescaleDB. If sustained breach detected → creates alert record, fires FCM push notification
Store
Reading written to sensor_readings hypertable in TimescaleDB. Partitioned by 7-day chunks. Compressed after 30 days. Retained for 3 years.
Surface
FastAPI serves live data to dashboard (30s refresh) and mobile app. WebSocket for real-time alert push. Nightly report job aggregates daily data → FSSAI PDF → AWS SES delivery at 00:05 AM.
Database strategy

The overarching storage architecture relies on a specialized approach: standard PostgreSQL handles the relational data (user accounts, alert rules, unit metadata), while TimescaleDB—a PostgreSQL extension—processes the massive influx of time-series sensor data.

The Storage Challenge
The primary hurdle in monitoring 7 units every minute is data volume: 10,080 rows a day, aggregating to over 3.7 million rows annually. TimescaleDB elegantly handles this through automatic time-based partitioning (chunking). By organizing data into 7-day intervals behind the scenes, queries remain remarkably fast. Older data is managed via compression policies after 30 days (reducing spatial footprint by ~90%), and automatically deleted after a 3-year compliance retention period.

The Export and Reporting Challenge
A key requirement was generating the FSSAI compliance report automatically at midnight. Calculating min/max temperatures and compliance percentages across millions of rows can easily lock up a standard database. By utilizing database read replicas and pre-calculated hourly roll-up tables, the nightly export completes in seconds without interrupting the 30-second live dashboard refresh cycle used by operations.

IoT Threshold Alerting
A distinct problem in IoT monitoring is that sensor data is inherently noisy. If an alert simply triggers when a temperature reading falls below or spikes above a threshold, normal warehouse operations (like briefly opening a freezer door to load stock) would flood technicians with false alarms. To combat this, the alert engine queries the TimescaleDB for continuous, rolling windows. A notification only fires if the temperature consistently breaches the threshold for a sustained period (e.g., 5-10 minutes), filtering out hardware glitches and brief drops, ensuring that when an alert does send, it represents a verified loss of temperature control.

Problems we ran into

Building on top of physical hardware means the real world pushes back in ways a purely software project doesn't. These were not edge cases we planned for. They were things we hit in practice and had to work through.

01
Dirty and missing sensor data. Sensors occasionally sent malformed payloads: truncated JSON, null values where a temperature reading should be, or timestamps that were clearly wrong. Early on we were writing these straight to the database, which meant the compliance report would sometimes show impossible values like 0.00°C on a freezer running at -20°C. We had to build a validation layer on the ingestion side that flagged bad readings, stored them separately with a quality marker, and excluded them from compliance calculations without dropping them entirely. They still needed to exist for audit purposes.
02
Sensors going offline mid-operation. A few ESP32 units lost Wi-Fi connectivity intermittently, usually during the peak heat of the afternoon when the warehouse was busiest. The gap in readings looked exactly like a sensor that had been physically damaged or switched off. We had to distinguish between "sensor is offline" and "sensor is reading something it shouldn't" because the response is completely different. One is a maintenance issue. The other is an emergency. We ended up adding a heartbeat check separate from the temperature readings, so the system could flag an offline sensor without triggering a cold chain alert.
03
Secure transmission was harder than expected. Sending sensor data over the local Wi-Fi to the gateway was fine. Getting it securely from the gateway to AWS IoT Core required device certificates, mutual TLS, and a proper certificate rotation process. In a SaaS product you'd handle this once. Here every physical device in the warehouse needed its own certificate, and if one expired silently the unit would stop reporting without any obvious error. We had to build certificate expiry monitoring into the system, which was not in the original spec.
04
Handling data volume at the query layer. The numbers sounded manageable in planning. 10,080 rows a day felt fine. But once we had a few months of data across 7 units, some of the dashboard queries started slowing down noticeably, especially the trend charts that were scanning large time ranges. TimescaleDB's chunking solved most of it, but we also had to add a pre-aggregated hourly rollup table so the 30-day chart wasn't recalculating from raw readings on every page load. That was a structural change we hadn't anticipated needing so soon.

None of these were blockers, but all of them added time and changed what we shipped in v1 versus what came later. The data quality work in particular was something we underestimated. Clean data sounds obvious. Getting it in practice, from hardware that occasionally misbehaves, in a warehouse that runs 24 hours a day, takes more thought than the happy path suggests.

What I learned

The most important product decision on this project happened before any requirement was written. We had to agree on what "failure" actually meant. The client came in wanting to monitor temperature. What they really needed was to close every gap in the continuous signal. That reframe changed the scope significantly and is what brought power monitoring and ATS integration in from day one rather than as an afterthought.

The 10-minute alert rule is a good example of where product thinking and technical thinking had to be in the same room. A simple implementation fires an alert the moment temperature crosses a threshold. In practice, that creates constant noise every time someone opens a door during normal loading, and the technician stops paying attention within a week. Getting the rule right meant understanding what actually happens on the floor, not just what the data model looks like.

The local resilience architecture was the hardest sell. Running alert logic on the edge gateway added complexity and cost, and it was a difficult conversation to justify. But the alternative was a system that could fail to protect stock at the exact moment the internet went down during a power event, which is precisely when it was needed most. Some technical decisions are really trust decisions. If the people relying on a system don't believe it will work when things go wrong, they won't rely on it when things go right either.
